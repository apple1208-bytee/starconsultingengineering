# Transient Surge Analysis of a 2 km LPG Unloading Pipeline

Fluid transients, commonly known as waterhammer or surge, represent one of the most severe loading conditions a piping system can experience. When moving fluid is abruptly forced to stop - such as during an Emergency Shutdown (ESD) valve closure or a sudden pump trip - the kinetic energy of the fluid instantly converts into strain energy, sending devastating shockwaves through the piping network. For an offshore import terminal handling Liquefied Petroleum Gas (LPG), a surge event doesn't just risk a ruptured pipe; it risks a catastrophic release of highly flammable, heavier-than-air vapor in a congested marine environment. This case study details the transient surge analysis and mechanical mitigation of a 2 km LPG unloading pipeline using AFT Impulse and CAESAR II.

## Why This Happens: The Mechanics of Fluid Transients

Surge is a dynamic phenomenon dictated by the Joukowsky equation. When a valve closes, the fluid immediately upstream compresses, and the pipe wall expands elastically. This creates a high-pressure wave that travels upstream at the acoustic velocity (speed of sound) of the fluid.

$$ \Delta P = \rho \cdot a \cdot \Delta V $$
*(Where $\Delta P$ is the pressure rise, $\rho$ is the fluid density, $a$ is the acoustic wave speed, and $\Delta V$ is the change in fluid velocity)*

For liquid LPG, the wave speed ($a$) is typically around 800 to 1,000 meters per second. If an LPG pipeline is flowing at 3 m/s and an ESD valve snaps shut instantaneously, the theoretical pressure spike can exceed 15 barg above the normal operating pressure.

However, surge is not just about the static bursting pressure. As the pressure wave reflects off elbows, tees, and the upstream pumps, it creates immense **unbalanced forces** on pipe segments. If the wave front hits the start of a 10-meter long pipe run, the pressure at the inlet of the run is instantaneously higher than the pressure at the outlet. This differential pressure creates a hammer-blow force along the axis of the pipe.

$$ F_{unbalanced} = (\Delta P_{inlet} - \Delta P_{outlet}) \times A_{internal} $$

These dynamic loads can rip pipe shoes off their structural steel, severely overload equipment nozzles, and cause catastrophic failure of bellows and flanges.

## Standards & Requirements: ASME B31.3 Occasional Loads

ASME B31.3 addresses fluid transients under "Occasional Loads" (Clause 302.3.6). Because surge events are rare and of very short duration, the code allows the longitudinal stress to temporarily exceed the basic allowable stress ($S_h$).

$$ S_{sus} + S_{occasional} \le 1.33 S_h $$

While the code allows a 33% increase in stress limits, it does not relax the allowable loads on connected equipment (like the marine loading arms or booster pumps), nor does it forgive the structural capacity of the pipe supports. The structural steel and pipe clamps must be capable of withstanding the peak dynamic force generated by the wave front.

## Methodology & Analysis Approach

The project involved a 16-inch, 2 km carbon steel (ASTM A333 Gr 6) pipeline transferring LPG from an offshore jetty to onshore storage spheres. The design pressure was 40 barg, operating at 15 barg and -40°C. The critical event was the spurious closure of the onshore ESD valve.

### Step 1: Hydraulic Transient Modelling (AFT Impulse)
To determine the magnitude of the pressure spike, the entire system was modeled in AFT (Applied Flow Technology) Impulse.
- **Fluid Properties:** Liquid Propane defined at -40°C. Bulk modulus and density were critical inputs for wave speed calculation.
- **System Geometry:** The 2 km line, including all elevation changes, was input to check for potential vapor cavity formation (column separation).
- **Valve Closure Profile:** The ESD valve stroke time was initially set to 3 seconds. The flow coefficient ($C_v$) vs. open percentage curve was accurately mapped, as the majority of flow restriction happens in the last 15% of valve closure.

### Step 2: Force Extraction
AFT Impulse identified the maximum pressure envelopes. More importantly, it calculated the transient unbalanced forces for every straight segment of pipe between direction changes. These force-time history files were exported for mechanical analysis.

### Step 3: Mechanical Dynamic Analysis (CAESAR II)
The piping system was modeled in Hexagon CAESAR II.
- Static cases (weight, thermal, pressure) were run first to establish the baseline state.
- A **Time History Dynamic Analysis** was executed. The force-time profiles from AFT Impulse were applied to the corresponding elbows in the 3D model.
- The dynamic solver calculated the transient displacements, maximum stresses, and peak support loads over a 10-second simulation window with a 0.005-second time step.

## Key Findings & Results Evaluation

The initial analysis revealed critical failures across the system:

*   **Extreme Pressure Spike:** The 3-second ESD valve closure generated a peak pressure of 48 barg at the valve face, exceeding the 40 barg design pressure.
*   **Unbalanced Forces:** The long, straight jetty approach experienced an unbalanced force of 145 kN (approx. 14.5 metric tons) along its axis.
*   **Support Failure:** The dynamic simulation in CAESAR II showed the pipe thrusting forward by 45 mm at a critical change of direction, completely shearing off the modeled guide supports and causing the occasional stress to reach 185% of the allowable limit.
*   **Marine Loading Arm Overload:** The reflected wave reached the jetty and transmitted a 35 kN bending force into the marine loading arm manifold, violating the vendor's strict allowable limits.

## Mitigation & Best Practices

To safely manage this surge event without redesigning the entire 2 km pipeline geometry, a multi-disciplinary mitigation strategy was required.

**1. Optimize Valve Closure Time (Hydraulic Fix):**
The most effective way to reduce surge is to increase the valve closure time. By negotiating with the safety instrumented system (SIS) engineers, the ESD closure time was extended from 3 seconds to 8 seconds. Furthermore, a two-stage closure profile was implemented: fast closure for the first 80% of the stroke, and a slow, damped closure for the final 20%. This single change reduced the peak pressure from 48 barg to 32 barg.

**2. Implement Axial Restraints (Mechanical Fix):**
To handle the remaining unbalanced forces (which still peaked at 65 kN), robust axial restraints (line stops) were introduced. However, placing a line stop on a -40°C cryogenic line restricts thermal contraction. The solution was to place the line stops exactly at the thermal neutral points of the piping loops, allowing the pipe to contract freely away from the stop while providing a rigid anchor against the sudden dynamic surge force.

**3. Upgraded Support Structures:**
Standard U-bolts were entirely inadequate. Heavy-duty, cryogenic trunnion supports with welded shear lugs were designed. The structural steel supporting these specific nodes was reinforced to absorb the 65 kN shock load without deflecting more than 2 mm.

## Conclusion

Transient surge events represent brief but violent anomalies that can effortlessly dismantle a poorly designed piping system. By coupling AFT Impulse's hydraulic precision with CAESAR II's mechanical dynamic solver, we were able to visualize the invisible shockwaves traveling through the 2 km LPG pipeline. The solution relied on a holistic approach: tweaking the control systems (valve timing) to reduce the source energy, and reinforcing the mechanical architecture (strategic line stops) to absorb the residual impact. Properly engineered, high-risk hydrocarbon offloading systems can achieve total compliance with ASME B31.3 occasional load criteria, ensuring that an emergency shutdown averts a disaster rather than causing one.
