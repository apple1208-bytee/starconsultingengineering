# Troubleshooting High-Frequency Vibration in Centrifugal Compressor Piping

When engineers discuss piping vibration, the focus immediately shifts to reciprocating machinery and low-frequency mechanical shaking. However, some of the most insidious and rapid failures occur on piping connected to high-speed centrifugal compressors or massive pressure let-down valves. These failures are characterized not by large, visible swaying of the pipe, but by high-frequency "screaming" vibration that can tear small-bore connections and instrument taps off the main header in a matter of hours. This case study explores a real-world troubleshooting intervention at a petrochemical complex, diagnosing and mitigating Flow-Induced Vibration (FIV) and Acoustic Induced Vibration (AIV).

## Why This Happens: FIV and AIV Mechanics

High-frequency vibration is typically driven by fluid flow phenomena rather than mechanical unbalance. 

**Flow-Induced Vibration (FIV):** 
FIV occurs when high-velocity fluid flows past an obstruction, such as a thermowell, a partially closed valve, or a dead-leg branch. As the fluid shears past the obstruction, it creates alternating vortices (Von Kármán vortex shedding). 

$$ f_v = \frac{S_t \cdot V}{D} $$
*(Where $f_v$ is the vortex shedding frequency, $S_t$ is the Strouhal number (typically ~0.2), $V$ is fluid velocity, and $D$ is the diameter of the obstruction)*

If this vortex shedding frequency aligns with the mechanical natural frequency of the obstruction (like a thermowell stem), it will violently vibrate and eventually snap off due to high-cycle fatigue.

**Acoustic-Induced Vibration (AIV):**
AIV is generated by immense sound power. When high-pressure gas is dropped across a pressure-reducing valve or a recycle valve, it generates broadband acoustic noise (1000 Hz to 5000+ Hz). This acoustic energy travels down the pipe, violently exciting the pipe wall itself. At these frequencies, the pipe behaves like a ringing bell. The high-frequency flexing of the pipe wall causes rapid fatigue failure at asymmetrical weld points, specifically at branch connections and welded supports. Energy Institute guidelines identify AIV as a primary cause of catastrophic, sudden piping failures.

## Standards & Requirements: Energy Institute Guidelines

ASME B31.3 primarily addresses static and low-frequency dynamic loads. For high-frequency AIV and FIV, the industry relies on the **Energy Institute (EI) Guidelines for the Avoidance of Vibration Induced Fatigue Failure in Process Pipework**.

The EI guidelines provide a quantitative screening method based on the Sound Power Level ($PWL$) generated by the valve or restriction. If the calculated $PWL$ exceeds 155 dB, the system is at high risk of AIV failure, and detailed finite element analysis (FEA) or heavy wall modifications are mandated.

## Methodology & Analysis Approach

The facility was experiencing recurrent failures of a 1-inch pressure transmitter tap located on the discharge header of a centrifugal recycle gas compressor. The failures were occurring at the weld root connecting the 1-inch thread-o-let to the 24-inch main header. 

### Step 1: Field Measurement & FFT Analysis
The first step in troubleshooting is not software, but field data. An accelerometer was placed on the failing branch connection, and a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) analyzer was used to capture the vibration spectrum.
- **Results:** The FFT revealed a massive vibration peak at 2,450 Hz, with very low amplitude at the 1X run speed of the compressor (which was ~150 Hz). This immediately ruled out mechanical unbalance from the compressor rotor.

### Step 2: Source Identification
With a 2,450 Hz signature, the issue was clearly acoustic. We mapped the piping upstream and identified the anti-surge (recycle) control valve located 10 meters away. By consulting the valve datasheet and applying the Energy Institute acoustic screening formula, we calculated the Sound Power Level ($PWL$) across the valve during peak recycle conditions. 

The $PWL$ was calculated at 162 dB - well into the danger zone for AIV.

### Step 3: Failure Mechanism
The intense acoustic energy from the recycle valve was causing the thin-walled 24-inch header pipe (Schedule 20) to vibrate radially (breathing mode) at high frequencies. The 1-inch instrument tap, welded to this flexing shell, acted as a rigid stress riser. The weld root was absorbing millions of micro-flexing cycles every hour, leading to rapid high-cycle fatigue cracking.

## Key Findings & Results Evaluation

The diagnostic phase confirmed that the failures were not due to poor welding or incorrect support spacing. The root cause was AIV generated by the anti-surge valve, which excited the main header's shell modes and concentrated fatigue stress at the small-bore connection.

Simply adding more pipe supports would not fix this. Standard pipe clamps only restrict low-frequency global swaying (1-20 Hz); they do absolutely nothing to stop the pipe wall from ringing at 2,450 Hz.

## Mitigation & Best Practices

Mitigating AIV requires either reducing the acoustic energy at the source or reinforcing the pipe wall to resist the acoustic fatigue.

**1. Source Mitigation (Valve Trim):**
The ideal solution is to replace the standard anti-surge valve trim with low-noise, multi-stage "whisper" trim. By dropping the pressure across a tortuous path of small holes rather than a single orifice, the acoustic energy shifts to even higher frequencies that are more easily absorbed by the gas and generates a lower overall Sound Power Level.

**2. Acoustic Reinforcement (The Immediate Fix):**
Because changing the valve required a lengthy shutdown and lead time, an immediate mechanical fix was required. The main header wall thickness was too thin to resist the acoustic energy. We designed and installed a heavy-wall reinforcement pad (a full wrap-around sleeve) at the location of the instrument tap. This localized thickening of the pipe wall shifted the local shell-mode natural frequency away from the acoustic excitation peak and drastically reduced the strain on the branch weld.

**3. Small-Bore Connection Redesign:**
The instrument tap was redesigned to be heavily braced. We replaced the thread-o-let with a heavy-duty weldolet and installed two-axis gusset plates tying the 1-inch valve body directly back to the reinforced 24-inch header shell. This ensured that the branch connection moved *with* the header wall, eliminating the differential flexing that was cracking the weld.

## Conclusion

High-frequency vibration issues like AIV and FIV require a completely different analytical approach than standard pipe stress or low-frequency shaking. Because acoustic failures occur incredibly fast - often within hours of startup - they must be designed out during the engineering phase using the Energy Institute screening guidelines. When troubleshooting in the field, FFT spectrum analysis is vital to distinguish between mechanical unbalance and acoustic resonance. By combining source-level noise reduction with heavily reinforced branch connections, engineers can permanently resolve these insidious, high-frequency failures.
