# When Piping Meets Pulsation: Preventing Vibrations in Reciprocating Compressor Systems

Reciprocating compressors are widely used across the oil, gas, petrochemical and process industries due to their ability to handle high pressures and variable operating conditions. However, these machines introduce a challenge that must be addressed carefully at the design stage: vibration in the connected piping system.

Unlike rotating equipment, reciprocating compressors generate cyclic pressure fluctuations as pistons move back and forth within the cylinders. These fluctuations are transmitted directly into the suction and discharge piping, creating pressure pulsations that travel through the system. If these pulsations are not properly controlled, they can excite acoustic resonance within the piping, leading to excessive vibration, fatigue damage, support failures and, in extreme cases, loss of containment.

## Why Piping Vibration Occurs

Reciprocating compressors are positive displacement machines. Every piston stroke generates a pressure wave that propagates through the connected piping. The piping system itself behaves like an acoustic circuit, with natural frequencies determined by its geometry, fluid properties and boundary conditions.

When the frequency of pressure excitation from the compressor aligns with one of the piping system's natural acoustic frequencies, resonance occurs. Under resonance conditions, even small pressure oscillations can be amplified significantly, resulting in large dynamic forces and vibration amplitudes.

These vibrations are not limited to the compressor nozzle. They can extend across long pipe runs, affect supports and structures, and induce fatigue damage at elbows, tees, reducers and small bore connections.

## Scope of Vibration and Pulsation Studies

The vibration issues discussed here arise specifically from acoustic effects generated by reciprocating compressors. Mechanical vibration sources such as rotating imbalance, coupling misalignment, foundation defects or lubrication problems are outside the scope of this discussion.

Because the compressor is the primary source of pulsation, its interaction with the connected piping must be evaluated under operating, transient and shutdown conditions. Acoustic modelling and pulsation studies are therefore essential inputs to piping layout, support design and overall system configuration.

## What Analyses Are Required

API 618, 8th Edition, Clause 7.9.4.2 defines the scope of pulsation and vibration studies for reciprocating compressors.

### Static Piping Stress Analysis

A static piping stress analysis, often referred to as equivalent static analysis, is performed for the discharge piping between the compressor and downstream equipment such as intercoolers or aftercoolers.

### Dynamic and Pulsation Analysis

Dynamic analysis focuses on the pressure pulsations generated by the compressor and their impact on the piping system.

#### Design Approach 1 (DA-1)

Empirical sizing of pulsation suppression devices such as bottles or dampeners.

#### Design Approach 2 (DA-2)

Detailed acoustic analysis to predict pressure pulsations, peak-to-peak amplitudes and shaking forces.

#### Design Approach 3 (DA-3)

Advanced analytical modelling techniques to predict pressure drop, acoustic behaviour and vibration response.

### Small Bore Connection Assessment

Small bore connections, typically 2-inch nominal pipe size and below, are particularly vulnerable to vibration-induced fatigue.

## Equivalent Static Piping Stress Analysis

Static stress analysis of piping connected to reciprocating compressors is carried out in accordance with ASME B31.3 using recognised tools such as CAESAR II.

[FIGURE 2: Schematic model of compressor skid]

The piping system and connected equipment are modelled with all relevant parameters, including material properties, pipe sizes, insulation, weights, supports, gaps, friction and anchor stiffness.

## Support Configuration, Clamp Behaviour and Stiffness Modelling

Support design plays a decisive role in controlling vibration in piping systems connected to reciprocating compressors.

Clamp-type supports are widely used to increase local stiffness and restrict excessive movement. By gripping the pipe and transferring loads more directly to the supporting structure, clamp supports help raise the mechanical natural frequency of the system and reduce vibration amplitudes.

[FIGURE 3: Caesar II model with clamp support]

The effective support stiffness is calculated using:

**1/Ktotal = 1/Kclamp + 1/Kstructure**

Where:

* Ktotal = Effective support stiffness
* Kclamp = Clamp stiffness
* Kstructure = Structural stiffness

## Stress Limits and Code Compliance

Static stress analysis differentiates between:

* Primary stresses
* Secondary stresses
* Occasional stresses

All stresses are evaluated in accordance with ASME B31.3 requirements.

## Modal Analysis and Frequency Separation

Modal analysis is essential for piping connected to reciprocating compressors. Industry practice targets a minimum natural frequency of 4.5 Hz, while API 618 recommends maintaining at least a 20% separation margin between excitation frequency and natural frequency.

## Pulsation and Shaking Force Evaluation

Pulsation analysis is performed using specialised software such as PULSIM following API 618 requirements.

Key outputs include:

* Pressure pulsation levels
* Shaking forces
* Pressure drop
* Vibration criteria
* Cyclic stress

[FIGURE 4: Shaking forces at elbows]

Excessive shaking forces may lead to increased vibration levels, fatigue failures and reduced system reliability.

## Pressure Drop and Vibration Limits

API 618 limits total pressure drop in the piping system to 0.25% of line pressure under rated conditions.

Typical allowable limits include:

* Constant vibration velocity: 32 mm/s peak-to-peak
* Maximum cyclic stress range: 180 N/mm²

## Small Bore Piping Connections

Recommended practices include:

* Using SCH 80 or higher pipe thickness
* Avoiding threaded fittings in vibration-prone areas
* Preferring weldolets and sweepolets
* Locating heavy components close to supports
* Minimising unsupported spans

## Mitigation and Best Practices

Effective vibration control relies on an integrated engineering approach:

* Independent modal analysis of suction and discharge piping
* Increasing natural frequency through support optimisation
* Avoiding small bore connections near turbulence sources
* Strengthening supports and structural elements
* Using pulsation suppression devices
* Optimising piping routing
* Installing anti-vibration clamp supports with rubber pads

## Conclusion

Vibration in piping systems connected to reciprocating compressors is a well-known but manageable engineering challenge. By combining static stress analysis, pulsation modelling, modal analysis and disciplined support design, vibration-related failures can be effectively avoided. A systematic engineering approach ensures improved reliability, operational safety and long-term performance of reciprocating compressor installations.
