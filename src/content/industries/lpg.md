Liquefied Petroleum Gas (LPG)—primarily consisting of propane, butane, or a mixture of the two—represents one of the most critical and challenging mediums in the global energy supply chain. The engineering of piping systems for LPG import terminals, massive sphere storage farms, and high-capacity jetty unloading facilities requires an intense focus on dynamic safety and precise material science. LPG exists as a gas at atmospheric pressure and ambient temperatures, meaning it must be maintained under substantial pressure or deep refrigeration to remain in its liquid state for transport and storage. A breach in an LPG piping system can result in massive vapor cloud expansions, severe fire hazards, and catastrophic boiling liquid expanding vapor explosions (BLEVE). Engineering these networks demands mastery over complex fluid dynamics, low-temperature material behavior, and extreme dynamic forces such as pressure surge. ## LPG Systems

An LPG terminal is a complex ecosystem of distinct but interconnected piping networks, each engineered for a specific phase of the product lifecycle. The journey typically begins with ship unloading operations at coastal marine facilities. Massive, high-capacity jetty transfer lines transport the pressurized or refrigerated liquid from the unloading arms across long trestles to the primary storage facility. These lines are subject to extreme environmental exposure, severe thermal contraction during chill-down, and the massive kinetic energy of high-velocity fluid transfer.

Once onshore, the product is routed into specialized containment systems, most commonly massive pressurized sphere storage tanks or elongated bullet tanks. The piping manifolds connecting these spheres require intricate design to balance flow, manage vapor return, and accommodate settlement or seismic movement of the massive vessels. For outbound logistics, the facility relies on intricate truck and rail loading gantries, which demand highly flexible piping designs and robust break-away safety mechanisms. Throughout all these operations, a dedicated vapor recovery system continuously manages the boil-off gas generated during transfer and storage, re-liquefying it or routing it safely. In fully refrigerated terminals handling massive volumes of pure propane, complex refrigeration loops must be engineered to continuously maintain the product at its boiling point (approximately -42°C / -44°F), demanding exhaustive thermal stress analysis and specialized insulation detailing.

## Pressure Surge Risk

Our signature capability in the LPG sector is the mitigation and management of pressure surge—commonly known as fluid hammer. Because LPG is pumped as a highly pressurized liquid at extreme velocities, any sudden change in fluid momentum generates massive, instantaneous pressure spikes that travel through the piping network at the speed of sound. The most dangerous scenario is an emergency shutdown (ESD) valve closure. When an automated ESD valve slams shut to isolate a leak, the kinetic energy of the flowing LPG impacts the closed valve, creating a devastating shockwave that can burst pipes, shatter flanges, and tear pipe supports from their foundations.

Similar, albeit less extreme, surges occur during a sudden pump trip or when an emergency release coupling (ERC) on a marine loading arm abruptly disconnects. These dynamic forces are complex, non-linear, and capable of destroying an improperly analyzed system in milliseconds. At Star Consulting, we utilize advanced transient fluid flow software to simulate these exact scenarios, providing comprehensive surge analysis capabilities. We calculate the exact magnitude and duration of the pressure spikes, verifying compliance with ASME B31.3 Clause 302.2.4, which dictates strict limits on occasional overpressure events. We then design the necessary surge mitigation strategies—such as accumulator sizing, valve closure timing optimization, and the design of ultra-rigid dynamic pipe restraints—to guarantee the structural survival of your terminal during an emergency shutdown.

## Material & Temperature

The thermodynamic properties of LPG dictate strict rules for material selection. While propane and butane can be stored at ambient temperatures under high pressure, sudden depressurization (such as a leak or blowdown event) causes the liquid to flash into vapor, rapidly drawing heat from its surroundings. This auto-refrigeration effect can plunge the local piping temperature down to -42°C (-44°F) for propane. Standard carbon steel becomes dangerously brittle at these sub-zero temperatures and is highly susceptible to catastrophic brittle fracture upon impact or sudden stress.

To combat this, LPG piping systems are predominantly engineered using specialized low-temp carbon steel, specifically grades like ASTM A333 Grade 6 for seamless and welded pipe. These materials are metallurgically formulated and heat-treated to maintain their toughness and ductility in freezing conditions. The design temperature for a typical pressurized propane system must be rated from a minimum of -48°C (-54°F) to account for auto-refrigeration, up to +65°C (+149°F) to account for maximum solar radiation absorption during peak summer operations. To verify the integrity of these materials, rigorous impact testing (such as the Charpy V-notch test) is mandated by code. Our engineers specify exact material grades, post-weld heat treatment requirements, and non-destructive examination protocols to ensure your piping network remains ductile and unbreakable across the entire spectrum of extreme operational temperatures.

## Safety & Codes

Safety is the paramount driver of all engineering decisions within an LPG facility, governed by a matrix of stringent international codes. The baseline mechanical design, wall thickness calculations, and stress limits for the piping itself are dictated by ASME B31.3 (Process Piping). However, the overarching safety layout and system architecture are heavily regulated by specialized industry standards. API 2510 (Design and Construction of LPG Installations) provides critical mandates regarding the spacing of sphere storage, the placement of fire protection systems, and the routing of emergency relief headers.

Furthermore, NFPA 58 (Liquefied Petroleum Gas Code) establishes the comprehensive safety requirements for the storage, handling, transportation, and use of LPG, particularly regarding safety instrumented systems and emergency shutoff protocols. For fully refrigerated, large-scale import terminals, API 620 governs the design of the massive low-pressure, low-temperature storage tanks and their associated piping interfaces. A critical component of this regulatory compliance is the design of overpressure protection. Our engineers meticulously size and route thermal relief valves (TRVs) and main pressure safety valves (PSVs), ensuring that any trapped liquid LPG expanding under solar heat, or vapor expanding during a fire scenario, is safely vented to a closed flare header system without exceeding the piping's maximum allowable working pressure.
